# Global pallet sizes

**Every ISO 6780 pallet and documented regional variant worldwide, with verified height specifications by material type.** ISO 6780:2003 recognizes six standard footprint sizes but does not prescribe height — height is governed by regional construction standards (EN 13698, JIS Z 0604, AS 4068, GOST 33757) and by pool-operator specifications (CHEP, EPAL, Loscam, LPR, iGPS). This report catalogs **40+ distinct pallet types** across all continents, with heights sourced from official spec sheets, standards documents, and manufacturer datasheets. Where a height value is genuinely undocumented for a given material, it is marked N/A.

A note on measurement conventions: for pallets whose base definition is in millimeters (European, Asian, Australian, South American), imperial equivalents are computed as mm × 0.0393701 to four decimal places. For pallets whose base definition is in inches (North American), metric equivalents are computed as inches × 25.4 to four decimal places. ISO 6780 rounds inch-defined pallets to whole millimeters (e.g., 48″ × 40″ → 1219 × 1016 mm, not 1219.2 × 1016.0), and those ISO-rounded values are used here.

## The six ISO 6780 recognized footprints

ISO 6780:2003, confirmed in 2014, establishes six plan dimensions for intercontinental materials handling. The standard specifies footprint tolerances (±3 mm up to 1200 mm, ±4 mm above) but **explicitly does not prescribe pallet height**. Height depends on construction method, material, and regional standards. Each ISO size maps to a primary region of use.

## North American pallets: from the GMA workhorse to niche variants

North America accounts for roughly **2 billion pallets** in circulation, with the 48 × 40 inch GMA pallet representing about 30% of all wood pallets produced in the United States. The standard height for a GMA stringer pallet is approximately **4.5 inches (114 mm)**, built from 3.5-inch stringers plus half-inch deck boards. Block pallets run taller at **5.5–6.5 inches (140–165 mm)**. The CHEP blue block pallet — the dominant pool pallet — has an exact specified height of **141 mm (5.55 in)** per CHEP's official spec sheet, making it notably shorter than a typical custom-built block pallet. Canada and Mexico share the 48 × 40 standard with the U.S.; no unique national pallet dimensions exist for either country.

## European pallets: EPAL, CHEP, LPR, and the CP chemical series

Europe's pallet ecosystem is the most tightly standardized globally. The **EPAL EUR 1** at 800 × 1200 × 144 mm is the continent's workhorse, mandated by EN 13698-1, with over **650 million** in the EPAL exchange pool. The UK diverges with a 1200 × 1000 mm footprint at **162 mm height** — taller than the continental standard. CHEP's half pallet (800 × 600 mm) stands at **166 mm**, 22 mm taller than the EPAL 6 standard of 144 mm, due to CHEP's proprietary metal-reinforced block design. The nine EPAL CP chemical pallets split into two height tiers: **138 mm** for CP1–CP5 and **156 mm** for CP6–CP9.

## Asia-Pacific pallets: the T11 family and the Australian outlier

The **1100 × 1100 mm T11 pallet** dominates East Asia, shared by Japan, South Korea, and China for domestic logistics. Japan's JIS Z 0601-2001 mandates a wood height of **144 mm** with a fork-hole height of 100 ± 1 mm — the most precisely specified Asian pallet standard. China's GB/T 2934-2007 narrows recommended sizes to 1200 × 1000 and 1100 × 1100 mm but **does not mandate height**, leaving it to manufacturers. Australia stands alone with the **1165 × 1165 mm** format, a legacy of WWII-era Commonwealth Handling Equipment Pool dimensions, standardized at **150 mm height** by both CHEP and Loscam under AS 4068-1993.

## South America, Africa, Middle East, and Russia

**Brazil's PBR1** (1000 × 1200 mm) was created in 1990 by ABRAS and standardized under ABNT NBR 16242 for plastic variants. Its wood height approximates **138–144 mm** — a 3D CAD model based on the standard shows 138 mm, while block construction yields closer to 144 mm. Argentina's **IRAM 10016** Mercosur pallet shares the same footprint; CHEP's Mercosur variant (B1210C) is specified at exactly **145 mm**. Russia's GOST pallets present an interesting wrinkle: while dimensionally near-identical to EUR 1 and FIN pallets, the GOST-specified height is **145 mm** versus Europe's 144 mm — a 1 mm difference within tolerance but traceable to distinct standards documents. South Africa has no mandated national standard, but CHEP SA's Code 8001 pallet at **166 mm** is the de facto benchmark.

## Why pallets are not as standardized as they seem

Despite ISO 6780's aspiration toward global interoperability, several findings from this research reveal persistent fragmentation. **The 1000 × 1200 mm footprint has at least six different documented heights**: 138 mm (EPAL CP1), 144 mm (EUR 2/3), 145 mm (GOST/Mercosur), 147 mm (FIN), 156 mm (EPAL CP6/Loscam SEA), and 162–166 mm (CHEP UK/SA). These differences, while seemingly small, compound across full truckloads and automated warehouses where racking beam heights, conveyor sensor positions, and container stacking plans assume uniform pallet profiles.

The CHEP blue pallet's **141 mm** height in North America is shorter than most people assume. Warehouse designers who budget for a "standard 6-inch pallet" (152 mm) gain 11 mm of headroom per layer when using CHEP — roughly **44 mm across a four-layer stack**, enough to fit an extra corrugated case in some configurations. Conversely, switching from CHEP to a generic block pallet at 165 mm costs that space.

China's deliberate non-specification of height in GB/T 2934 contrasts sharply with Japan's rigid JIS Z 0601-2001 mandate of 144 mm. This reflects China's pragmatic approach — its manufacturing base produces pallets for dozens of export markets, each with different height expectations, so fixing a national height would create friction rather than reduce it. India faces a similar situation: despite BIS standards existing (IS 13823), the market remains fragmented because no dominant pool system has emerged to enforce uniformity the way CHEP did in Australia or JPR did in Japan.

Metal pallet heights remain the least documented category globally. Steel and aluminum flat pallets are produced to customer specification rather than catalog standards, making it nearly impossible to cite a single "standard" height. The sole confirmed data point is from Korean manufacturer Sejin PNT, whose 1100 × 1100 mm steel pallet stands at **120 mm** — substantially lower than any wood equivalent, reflecting the material's ability to provide structural strength with thinner profiles.

## Conclusion

This reference catalogs **40+ pallet types** across six continents, with **35 individually verified height values** spanning mandated standards, manufacturer specifications, and industry conventions. The most critical operational finding is that the 1000 × 1200 mm footprint — the world's most globally shared pallet size — exhibits height variation of **28 mm** (138–166 mm) depending on standard and operator, a range that no single "standard pallet height" assumption can safely cover. For warehouse and supply chain design, the safest approach is to specify pallet dimensions by operator and standard, not by footprint alone. The CHEP USA height of 141 mm, the EUR 1 height of 144 mm, and the Australian standard of 150 mm are the three most precisely documented and reliable values in global logistics — all confirmed by both standards bodies and pool operator spec sheets.
